Nomad Interview · Issue No.5

What Is Life

Life seeks hope. Every living being endures uncertainty and moves forward in search of it. This issue explores what life is—across science, technology, and humanity.

“Emotion, love, empathy, kindness. These are not concepts that exist at a fundamental level.
These are concepts that emerge when you get to a scale of roughly one meter.
But they’re not concepts that are there at 10-35 meters—the fundamental length scale of the universe.

And so, if you want to find something on which to rest the principles by which you live your life,
I think fundamental physics is not the thing to look to—
unless you just want to accept that the universe doesn’t care about you.

That’s the lesson: the universe doesn’t care about humans. It does not care about our existence.
The fact that we do exist is, for want of a better word, a miracle.
And it’s clear that what’s happening here on Earth—because of our existence—is
significantly more complex than anything else we’ve ever discovered in the universe.

That makes it interesting. That also makes it precious. And we should care for it.”

David Tong
Professor of Theoretical Physics, Trinity College, Cambridge

About This Issue

Life seeks hope.
Every living being endures uncertainty and moves forward in search of it.

Born from fear, this journey explores what life is—
through the vertical axis of scientific and technological inquiry,
spanning quantum physics, advanced technologies, and cosmology,
and the horizontal axis of human reflection in philosophy, art, and religion.

As these axes intersect,
the meaning of life emerges,
growing deeper and wider in our understanding.

Inside

Science

Fear led the question inward—toward the smallest scales of existence.
From Schrödinger’s radical inquiry What Is Life? to contemporary quantum physics, this section traces how uncertainty and entanglement may govern not only particles, but life itself.

Technology

At the edge where theory becomes tool, quantum mechanics transforms into technology.
From computation to communication, we explore how the properties of light and matter give rise to machines that extend perception, intelligence, and possibility.

Humanity

Beyond equations and devices lies another axis: human reflection.
Through conversations with philosophers, artists, and spiritual thinkers, this section confronts fear, meaning, and connection—asking how we might walk forward together in an entangled world.
